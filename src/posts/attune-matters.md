The most-watched ongoing event in CNS antisense is a trial that almost nobody outside the rare-disease community has heard of. Ionis is running ATTUNE — NCT06430385, Phase 1/2 — for boys aged 2 to 17 with MECP2 duplication syndrome. The drug, ION440, is an intrathecal antisense oligonucleotide that lowers MECP2 expression back toward wild-type. The trial is enrolling. Top-line readout is expected sometime in 2027.

For our program, this is the closest analog in the entire pipeline of human medicine.

MECP2 duplication and 15q11.2 BP1–BP2 duplication are not the same disease. The genes are different, the chromosomes are different, the syndromes look different. But the underlying drug-development thesis is identical: a recurrent copy-number variant produces a single gene overexpressed by roughly 50%, the phenotype tracks dosage, and the obvious intervention is to lower that gene back toward normal with an intrathecally delivered ASO. If you squint, both programs are testing the same hypothesis at the level of the drug class: *can intrathecal ASO knockdown of an overexpressed neurodevelopmental gene safely produce clinical benefit in pediatric patients?*

If ATTUNE reads out positive, the question is settled for the class. Not for the specific molecule, but for the modality — the regulatory path, the dosing schedule, the safety profile of repeated intrathecal injections in young children, the kinds of endpoints FDA will accept. Every duplication-syndrome program that follows benefits from the precedent.

If ATTUNE reads out negative — meaning safe but not efficacious — the calculus shifts in a different direction. The interpretation matters: was the knockdown insufficient, was the timing wrong, was the readout underpowered, were the endpoints wrong? Each of those failure modes has different implications. A clean negative on a well-powered primary endpoint with good knockdown suggests the dosage-correction thesis itself is incomplete, which would force a rethink for every program built on it. A negative driven by underpowered endpoints or insufficient knockdown is a different story — it tells you the modality works, but the trial design needs to be different.

The third scenario, which the field tends not to discuss, is that the trial reads out *ambiguous*. A signal on one endpoint, no signal on another, with confidence intervals that include zero. This is by far the most likely outcome for a small Phase 1/2 in a rare neurodevelopmental population. It is also the hardest outcome to act on. Programs that are waiting for ATTUNE will have to make a decision under continued uncertainty, with whatever the trial does provide as the best available data.

We have built our program plan around this reality. The Go/No-Go gate for our preclinical CYFIP1 program is set at month 24 — late enough in 2027 that ATTUNE topline is likely available, but not dependent on it. If ATTUNE reads positive before then, it accelerates our pharma-partnership conversations. If it reads negative, we go into our own dose-response data with much sharper questions about what the failure mode implies for us. If it reads ambiguous, we do what most rare-disease programs do, which is keep working from our own data and let the field figure it out.

What the program cannot do is wait. Every quarter we delay is a quarter that patients with the indication get older, that the regulatory window shifts under us, and that the priority-review-voucher economics that fund the late-stage trial get less favorable. The PRV program is currently authorized through September 2029. A reauthorization is plausible but not certain. The companies that will be in the best position when ATTUNE reads out are the ones that already have their own preclinical data package ready to go.

Ionis is, in effect, paying for the most expensive de-risking experiment in our field, and we get the result for free. The right response is to be ready to act on it the day it lands.
