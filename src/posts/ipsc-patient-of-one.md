---
title: "iPSC Neurons as a Patient-of-One Validation Model"
date: "2026-06-05"
excerpt: "The hardest engineering problem in a rare-disease program is that you cannot test the drug on the patient before you give it to them. iPSC-derived cortical neurons from the patient's own skin cells are the closest available substitute, and the only one that runs on human biology with the patient's exact genetic background."
category: "therapeutics"
---

The classical drug development funnel runs cells, then mice, then non-human primates, then humans. The cells are usually an immortalized line — HeLa, SH-SY5Y, HEK293 — that is convenient to grow but has nothing in common with the patient's biology except being human. The mice are usually engineered to express the human target, but on a mouse genetic background that responds differently to almost every drug. By the time you get to humans, you have made several conceptual jumps, and the failure rate at the human-translation step is famously high.

For a recurrent copy-number variant like 15q11.2 BP1–BP2 microduplication, this funnel has a curious property: you can shortcut it. The patient's own cells are accessible, the genetic lesion is present in every one of them, and the in-vitro biology that matters most for our program — neuronal dendritic morphology, synaptic transmission, translation regulation downstream of CYFIP1 — can be reconstituted in a dish.

The protocol is well-established. A skin punch biopsy or a peripheral blood draw provides fibroblasts or PBMCs. Those cells are reprogrammed back to a pluripotent state using the standard Yamanaka cocktail of transcription factors, producing patient-specific induced pluripotent stem cells. The iPSCs are then differentiated, over roughly 30 to 60 days depending on protocol, into cortical neurons that exhibit the synaptic and morphological hallmarks of mature neurons. Every cell in the dish carries the patient's exact genome, including the duplication.

For our program, this is the most important preclinical asset we will build.

The reason is that the dose-response window I wrote about previously is not a single window — it is a *patient-specific* window. The amount of knockdown that produces meaningful rescue without phenotypic encroachment depends on the patient's baseline CYFIP1 expression, their genetic modifiers, the methylation state of their imprinted regions, the expression of the other three genes in the duplicated interval. The window in a *Cyfip1*-overexpressing mouse is informative about the existence of a window. The window in a patient's own neurons is informative about *that patient's* window.

This matters operationally in two ways. First, it lets us select ASO candidates against the right biological target. A sequence that achieves 50% knockdown in a generic line might achieve 35% or 65% in patient neurons, and the slope of the dose-response curve is what determines the safety margin. We want to make that selection on cells that look as much like the patient's own neurons as possible. Second, it provides a pathway for individualized therapy. If our lead ASO produces a clean dose-response in patient neurons from one family but a flat response from another, that is a signal worth acting on — possibly a different ASO, possibly a different dose, possibly a different patient.

The infrastructure to do this work seriously is concentrated in a small number of academic laboratories. The Centre for Genomic Regulation in Barcelona has been doing iPSC neuronal differentiation at scale for over a decade. The Erasmus MC group around Ype Elgersma has developed xenotransplantation protocols that let human iPSC-derived neurons grow in mouse brain — a hybrid system that captures human biology at the cellular level inside a real-tissue context. The Nadif Kasri lab at Radboud has been doing E/I balance work in patient-derived neurons specifically for neurodevelopmental conditions. Any one of these is a credible partner for a sponsored research arrangement.

The reason the iPSC track is undervalued in industry programs is that it is slow and expensive on a per-experiment basis. Differentiation takes weeks, the cells are noisy, the throughput is low compared to immortalized lines. None of these objections matter for a rare-disease program where the patient population is small enough that you will be running thousands of patients, not millions. The throughput you need is bounded by the trial size, not by the mass market. The validation you get in exchange is the only validation that operates on the actual disease biology.

In a program where the target is one of the most dosage-sensitive genes in the genome and the population is one of the most heterogeneous, anything less than patient-derived validation is leaving the most important question — *will this work on the patient's own neurons* — for the trial itself to answer. Trials answer questions much more expensively than dishes do.
