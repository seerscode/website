title: "The U-Shape of CYFIP1 Dosage"
date: "2026-05-31"
excerpt: "CYFIP1 deletion causes disease. CYFIP1 duplication also causes disease. The therapeutic window for a knockdown program sits in a narrow band between two cliffs — and characterizing that window honestly is the most important thing a CYFIP1 drug developer has to do."
category: "therapeutics"
---

The cleanest first question I had to answer about CYFIP1 was a question I would rather not have asked: how much knockdown is the right amount?

For most antisense programs, more knockdown is monotonically better. SMN2 in spinal muscular atrophy — the more transcript you upregulate, the more motor neurons survive. HTT in Huntington's — the more you lower the mutant allele, the less aggregation. Even in MECP2 duplication, the target of the Ionis ATTUNE trial, the goal is to bring overexpression back toward wild-type and stop there. The risk profile is asymmetric: under-dose and you get nothing, over-dose and you waste drug.

CYFIP1 is not that. CYFIP1 is one of a small family of genes where dosage matters in *both* directions. Deletion of the same 15q11.2 BP1–BP2 interval that contains CYFIP1 produces Burnside-Butler syndrome — a phenotype that overlaps significantly with the duplication phenotype on autism, anxiety, language delay, and motor coordination. The Edinburgh group around Peter Kind has shown that *Cyfip1+/−* mice have abnormal dendritic spine morphology and impaired synaptic plasticity. The Bagni lab has shown that CYFIP1 sits at the center of two pathways — the FMRP-eIF4E translational brake and the WAVE actin regulatory complex — and that both pathways are exquisitely sensitive to CYFIP1 abundance.

So the therapeutic window for a CYFIP1-lowering ASO is bracketed by two failure modes. Under-knock and you fail to correct the duplication phenotype. Over-knock and you produce the deletion phenotype. The window in between, if it exists at all, is what the program has to characterize before any human ever gets dosed.

The first preclinical experiment that matters, then, is not "does the ASO work" but "what is the dose-response curve." A full titration in the CYFIP1 overexpression mouse model, with multiple intermediate doses spanning roughly 15% to 75% knockdown, mapped against a *Cyfip1+/−* haploinsufficiency comparator on the same behavioral and electrophysiological readouts. If there is a clean window — meaningful rescue at, say, 30% knockdown without phenotypic encroachment toward the deletion side — the program proceeds. If the windows overlap, the program ends.

This is the kind of experiment that costs roughly two million dollars and takes eighteen months to do properly. It is the single highest-information experiment in the entire program plan. Most drug development burns capital trying to optimize a candidate before the basic dose-response question is settled. Inverting that order — settling the dose-response question first, and only investing in candidate optimization if the window exists — is the only honest way to run a CYFIP1 program.

The corollary is that the choice of modality matters enormously. ASOs are the right first choice precisely because they are dose-titratable and reversible. You can adjust dose, you can stop dosing. AAV-delivered RNAi, by contrast, gives you a permanent reduction with no titration after administration. CRISPRi epigenetic silencing has the same problem in a different form. Neither is appropriate as a first-in-human modality for a U-shaped target, because you cannot back out of a wrong dose. ASOs let you make a mistake without making it permanent.

The framing I keep coming back to is that the most important deliverable in the first two years of this program is not a drug. It is a dose-response curve clean enough to decide whether the drug is worth pursuing. If we generate that curve and the window is clean, we have de-risked the entire indication. If the window is not clean, we have saved everyone — patients, investors, ourselves — from a decade of expensive failure.

Either outcome is a real result. The thing you cannot do honestly is skip the experiment.
